Dive Computers have become a standard piece of scuba diving equipment in recent years, tracking inert gas loading and corresponding decompression obligation, as well as oxygen intake and the associated limits.
These battery-powered and life support equipment systems are often developed in a commercial setting with not all research available to the public.
To save energy in low-power devices, research has shown that instead of sampling and running algorithms at fixed rates, variable rate approaches can decrease battery usage or enable fully self-sufficient devices, while the results diverge, with the reduction in cycles for the computations or increase in samplings reaching from $2.06\times$ to $567\times$, based on the metric and application investigated in the specific article.
Current dive computers are based on a few different algorithms, but only a US Navy proprietary dive computer uses a linear-exponential algorithm, which has been shown to lower DCS risk on deeper dives.

This work presents an extendable Open Source hardware and software basis for the development of dive computers that is feasible to build and extend for non-professionals, while remaining small enough to be worn during a scuba dive.
The diving-related algorithms for inert gas loading, decompression, and oxygen toxicity tracking are implemented in a separate Open Source module.
Multiple algorithms are implemented and used for the variable rate scheduling of different tasks in dive mode.
These algorithms show a reduction in executions from $1.17\times$ to $6\times$ for decompression calculations without significant difference in the resulting simulated dive profiles, while the oxygen toxicity calculation does not benefit from the current implementation of variable rate scheduling.
The usage of the Thalmann-inspired linear-exponential algorithm leads to slightly deeper stops, but is not more expensive in terms of compute.
